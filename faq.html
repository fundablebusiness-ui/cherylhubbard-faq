Fundability

Recommended Page: Home

What is fundability?

Short Answer: Fundability is how qualified and lendable a business looks to banks, lenders, and investors based on factors like business credit, financial documentation, revenue history, time in business, and legal structure.

Detailed Answer: Many owners assume strong revenue automatically qualifies them for funding. In reality, lenders evaluate fundability indicators: personal and business credit profiles, cash flow consistency, time in business, industry risk classification, documentation readiness, and entity structure.

Example: A retail business generating $15,000/month may qualify for very different funding products than a startup with strong projections but no revenue history.

Next Step: A fundability assessment can identify specific gaps before you apply.

What is a Fundability Score?

Short Answer: A Fundability Score is a way of measuring how well a business meets the factors lenders and investors evaluate, business credit, documentation, revenue, and structure, condensed into a single readiness indicator.

Detailed Answer: Unlike a personal or business credit score alone, a Fundability Score accounts for the full picture lenders assess. It's used as a diagnostic starting point in ShesFundable's assessments and the Funding Readiness Blueprint to prioritize which gaps to fix first.

Next Step: Ask about a Fundability Score assessment through the Booking page.

How do I improve my business's fundability?

Short Answer: You improve fundability by building separate business credit, organizing financial documentation, maintaining consistent revenue records, and ensuring your legal entity structure matches what lenders expect for your industry and funding type.

Detailed Answer: Improvement usually follows a sequence: first close documentation gaps (financial statements, tax filings, business licenses), then build a business credit profile distinct from personal credit, then address revenue consistency and time-in-business factors. Each funding type weighs these differently, so the right order depends on which funding you're pursuing.

Common Mistake: Trying to fix everything at once instead of prioritizing the highest-impact gaps first.

Next Step: Start with a fundability assessment to identify your highest-priority gaps.

What is a fundability assessment?

Short Answer: A fundability assessment is an evaluation of a business across the specific factors lenders and investors consider, credit, documentation, revenue, and structure, producing a prioritized list of gaps to close before applying for funding.

Detailed Answer: Assessments typically review business and personal credit reports, financial statements, entity documentation, and industry classification, then compare findings against the requirements of common funding types the business is likely to pursue.

Next Step: Schedule a fundability assessment through the Booking page.

What is the difference between fundability and creditworthiness?

Short Answer: Creditworthiness is one component of fundability. Fundability is the broader combination of credit, documentation, revenue history, and structure that determines whether a business qualifies for funding overall.

Detailed Answer: A business can have excellent personal credit and still be unfundable due to missing financial documentation, no separate business credit profile, or an entity structure lenders consider high risk. Fundability looks at the whole picture, not credit alone.

Funding Readiness

Recommended Page: Home / Media & Resources

What is funding readiness?

Short Answer: Funding readiness is the state of having your business credit, documentation, financials, and structure prepared well enough to confidently apply for and qualify for capital, rather than applying reactively when cash is needed.

Detailed Answer: Funding readiness is the practical, action-oriented companion to fundability. While fundability describes the factors lenders evaluate, funding readiness describes the process of actually getting those factors in order before you need capital urgently.

Common Mistake: Waiting until a cash crunch to start preparing, which limits both options and negotiating power.

Next Step: The Funding Readiness Blueprint walks through this preparation step by step.

How do I know if my business is funding ready?

Short Answer: A business is funding ready when its credit profile, financial documentation, revenue history, and legal structure all align with the requirements of the funding type it intends to pursue.

Detailed Answer: Readiness is funding-type-specific: a business ready for a line of credit may not yet be ready for an SBA loan, which has stricter documentation and time-in-business requirements. A readiness assessment compares your current standing against the requirements of multiple funding types.

Next Step: Request a funding readiness assessment through the Booking page.

What is a funding readiness checklist?

Short Answer: A funding readiness checklist outlines the specific documents, credit benchmarks, and structural requirements a business should confirm before applying for capital, reducing the risk of denial.

Detailed Answer: A typical checklist covers: separate business bank account and EIN, 2+ years of financial statements or tax returns where applicable, an established business credit profile, clear revenue documentation, and confirmation of entity structure appropriate for the funding type sought.

How long does it take to become funding ready?

Detailed Answer: Businesses with an existing track record and clean documentation may only need to address a few specific gaps, while newer businesses may need more time to build business credit history and establish consistent revenue records before applying.

Business Funding

Recommended Page: Home

What is business funding?

Short Answer: Business funding is money provided to a business through loans, grants, investors, lines of credit, or alternative financing sources, used for operations, growth, or specific business needs.

Detailed Answer: Funding falls into broad categories: debt financing (loans, lines of credit), equity financing (investors), and non-dilutive financing (grants, some government programs). Each comes with different qualification requirements, repayment expectations, and impact on ownership.

Next Step: See the Funding Types FAQs to compare specific options.

What types of business funding are available?

Short Answer: Common types of business funding include SBA loans, microloans, lines of credit, business credit cards, equipment financing, invoice financing, revenue-based financing, merchant cash advances, term loans, grants, and crowdfunding.

Detailed Answer: Each funding type fits a different business profile. SBA loans and term loans typically suit established businesses with strong documentation; microloans and CDFI loans often serve newer or underserved businesses; revenue-based and invoice financing suit businesses with strong cash flow but thinner credit history.

Next Step: A funding readiness assessment can help match your business to the right funding type.

How does business funding work?

Short Answer: Business funding works by a lender, investor, or grant program evaluating a business's fundability, then providing capital under terms specific to that funding type, ranging from repayment schedules to equity stakes or grant compliance requirements.

Detailed Answer: The process generally follows: application, documentation review, underwriting or evaluation, approval or denial, and funding disbursement. The specifics, required documents, timelines, and approval criteria, vary significantly by funding type and lender.

Next Step: Review the Funding Readiness FAQs to prepare for this process.

How much revenue do I need to qualify for business funding?

Short Answer: Revenue requirements vary by funding type, but many traditional lenders look for at least $10,000 to $15,000 in average monthly revenue, while some alternative and grant-based options have lower or no revenue thresholds.

Detailed Answer: Revenue-based financing and merchant cash advances often have lower entry thresholds but higher costs, while SBA loans and traditional bank financing typically require more established, documented revenue. Startups without revenue often pursue grants, angel investment, or microloans instead.

Common Mistake: Assuming revenue is the only qualifying factor; lenders also weigh credit, time in business, and documentation.

Next Step: A fundability assessment will show which funding types match your current revenue profile.

What is the difference between debt financing and equity financing?

Short Answer: Debt financing involves borrowing money that must be repaid with interest, while equity financing involves giving up partial ownership in exchange for capital with no repayment obligation.

Detailed Answer: Debt financing (loans, lines of credit) preserves ownership but requires regular repayment regardless of business performance. Equity financing (angel investors, venture capital) removes repayment pressure but dilutes ownership and often involves investor input in decisions.

Next Step: Discuss which financing path fits your goals during a consultation.

Access to Capital

What does access to capital mean?

Short Answer: Access to capital refers to a business's ability to obtain funding, loans, grants, investment, or credit, when needed, and is often used to describe systemic gaps that make this harder for certain groups of business owners.

Detailed Answer: Access to capital isn't just about whether funding exists; it's about whether a specific business or business owner can realistically qualify for and obtain it given their credit history, documentation, network, and industry.

Why is access to capital harder for some entrepreneurs?

Detailed Answer: These gaps aren't usually about business viability; they're about preparation and positioning. A profitable business can still struggle to access capital if it hasn't built business credit, organized documentation, or established a track record lenders recognize.

Detailed Answer: These programs often have more flexible underwriting criteria than traditional bank financing, but they still require documentation and a baseline level of fundability to qualify. Pairing program awareness with fundability preparation produces the strongest results.

How can I improve my access to capital as a small business owner?

Business Credit

Recommended Page: Home / Media & Resources

What is business credit?

Short Answer: Business credit is a credit profile tied to your business as a legal entity, separate from your personal credit, used by lenders and vendors to evaluate your business's creditworthiness and payment history.

Detailed Answer: Business credit is built through an EIN, a business bank account, trade lines with vendors and suppliers, and credit accounts opened in the business's name, and is tracked by agencies such as Dun & Bradstreet, Experian Business, and Equifax Business.

Next Step: See the Business Credit Building FAQ for how to start establishing this profile.

How do I build business credit?

Short Answer: You build business credit by establishing your business as a separate legal entity, opening trade accounts with vendors that report payments, obtaining a business credit card, and maintaining consistent, on-time payment history.

Detailed Answer: The process typically starts with an EIN and business bank account, followed by establishing vendor trade lines that report to business credit bureaus, then adding business credit cards or small credit lines. Consistency and time matter as much as the accounts themselves.

Common Mistake: Using only personal credit for business expenses, which does nothing to build a separate business credit profile.

Next Step: A business credit strategy session can map out your specific building plan.

What is the difference between business credit and personal credit?

Short Answer: Personal credit reflects an individual's borrowing history and is tied to a Social Security number, while business credit reflects a business entity's borrowing and payment history and is tied to an EIN.

Detailed Answer: Lenders often evaluate both when a business is newer or smaller, but as a business matures, a strong business credit profile reduces reliance on the owner's personal credit and can unlock funding amounts and terms personal credit alone wouldn't support.

Next Step: Learn how both profiles factor into fundability in the Fundability FAQs.

What is a good business credit score?

Short Answer: Business credit scores vary by bureau and scoring model, but generally a Dun & Bradstreet PAYDEX score of 80 or higher, or an Experian Business score above 76, is considered strong and favorable to lenders.

Detailed Answer: Unlike personal credit scores, business credit scores often range differently by bureau and weigh payment timeliness heavily. A strong score typically reflects consistent on-time payments across multiple trade lines over time.

Next Step: Check your business credit reports across major bureaus to see where you currently stand.

How does business credit affect funding approval?

Short Answer: Business credit affects funding approval by giving lenders an independent view of your business's payment reliability, separate from personal credit, often influencing both approval odds and the funding amount or terms offered.

Detailed Answer: A weak or nonexistent business credit profile forces lenders to rely more heavily on personal credit and collateral, which can limit funding options. A strong business credit profile can open access to higher limits, better rates, and funding types reserved for established businesses.

Next Step: A fundability assessment will show how your current business credit profile affects your funding options.

Funding Denials

Recommended Page: Home / Media & Resources

Why do businesses get denied funding?

Short Answer: Businesses are commonly denied funding due to thin or inconsistent business credit, insufficient documentation, low time in business, inconsistent revenue, or a legal structure that doesn't match the lender's risk criteria.

Detailed Answer: Denial is rarely about one single factor; it's usually a combination of smaller gaps that compound. A business might have decent revenue but missing financial statements, or strong personal credit but no business credit profile at all, each of which raises red flags during underwriting.

Next Step: A fundability assessment identifies which specific factors are likely contributing to denials.

Why was my business loan denied?

Short Answer: A business loan denial usually points to one or more underlying fundability gaps, weak business credit, missing documentation, insufficient revenue history, or a legal structure or industry classification the lender considers high risk.

Detailed Answer: Lenders are generally required to provide a reason for denial, which can offer a starting point. However, the stated reason often reflects only the most prominent gap, not the full picture of what needs to improve before reapplying.

Common Mistake: Reapplying immediately with another lender without addressing the underlying gap, which usually leads to another denial.

Next Step: Use a fundability assessment to identify the full set of gaps before reapplying.

How can I improve my approval odds after a funding denial?

Short Answer: You improve approval odds after a denial by identifying the specific fundability gaps that caused it, addressing them systematically, and waiting an appropriate amount of time before reapplying with stronger documentation.

Detailed Answer: This typically means reviewing the denial reason, auditing your business and personal credit, organizing financial documentation, and confirming your funding request matches what your current fundability profile supports, rather than reapplying for the same amount through the same type of lender.

Next Step: Schedule a consultation to map a recovery plan after a denial.

What are common lender red flags?

Short Answer: Common lender red flags include inconsistent revenue deposits, missing or disorganized financial statements, high credit utilization, recent late payments, frequent credit inquiries, and a legal structure or industry classified as high risk.

Detailed Answer: Lenders look for predictability and documentation clarity. Red flags often aren't dealbreakers individually, but several together signal higher risk and can tip a borderline application toward denial.

Next Step: A fundability assessment flags these red flags before you apply, not after a denial.

Lender Requirements

Recommended Page: Media & Resources

What documents do lenders require for business funding?

Short Answer: Lenders commonly require business financial statements, tax returns, bank statements, a business plan or use-of-funds statement, proof of business registration, and personal and business credit reports.

Detailed Answer: Requirements vary by funding type and lender size. Traditional banks and SBA lenders typically require more extensive documentation, including multiple years of tax returns and detailed financials, while some alternative lenders require less paperwork in exchange for higher costs.

Next Step: A funding readiness checklist can help you organize these documents in advance.

How do lenders evaluate business risk?

Short Answer: Lenders evaluate business risk through underwriting criteria including credit history, cash flow consistency, debt service coverage, time in business, industry risk classification, and collateral availability.

Detailed Answer: Underwriting is essentially a risk-scoring process: lenders weigh how likely a business is to repay based on historical patterns and current financial health. Industries classified as higher risk, or businesses with inconsistent cash flow, often face stricter requirements or higher costs.

Next Step: Learn how to address these risk factors in the Fundability FAQs.

What is underwriting in business lending?

Short Answer: Underwriting is the process lenders use to evaluate a loan application's risk by reviewing credit history, financial statements, cash flow, and other fundability factors before approving or denying funding.

Detailed Answer: Underwriting can be automated (algorithm-driven, common with smaller alternative loans) or manual (a human underwriter reviewing documentation in depth, common with SBA and traditional bank loans). Each approach weighs fundability factors somewhat differently.

Next Step: Prepare for underwriting with a fundability assessment before applying.

What financial ratios do lenders look at?

Short Answer: Lenders commonly evaluate debt service coverage ratio, current ratio, debt-to-income ratio, and gross margin to assess a business's ability to manage and repay additional debt.

Detailed Answer: These ratios help lenders translate raw financial statements into a risk picture. A low debt service coverage ratio, for example, signals that a business may struggle to make loan payments on top of existing obligations, even if revenue looks strong on paper.

Next Step: Review your financial ratios with a fundability assessment before applying for funding.

Financial Literacy

Recommended Page: Speaking / Media & Resources

What is financial literacy for entrepreneurs?

Short Answer: Financial literacy for entrepreneurs is the practical understanding of how to manage business finances, including cash flow, budgeting, financial statements, credit, and funding, well enough to make sound business decisions.

Detailed Answer: Beyond personal budgeting, entrepreneur financial literacy includes reading and interpreting financial statements, understanding how lenders evaluate a business, and knowing how financing decisions affect long-term business health and ownership.

Next Step: Cheryl speaks on financial literacy topics at conferences and through ShesFundable Studio.

Why is financial literacy important for small business owners?

Short Answer: Financial literacy is important for small business owners because it directly affects funding readiness, day-to-day cash flow management, and the ability to make informed decisions about growth, credit, and risk.

Detailed Answer: Businesses that lack financial literacy often struggle not because of poor performance, but because of poor financial visibility, missed documentation, inconsistent record-keeping, or misunderstanding how lenders and investors evaluate their numbers.

Next Step: Explore financial literacy resources through ShesNewsy and ShesFundable Studio.

What financial statements should every small business maintain?

Short Answer: Every small business should maintain a profit and loss statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement at minimum, updated regularly enough to support both daily decision-making and funding applications.

Detailed Answer: These three statements together give a complete financial picture: profitability over time, what the business owns and owes, and how cash actually moves in and out. Lenders almost universally request some version of these documents during underwriting.

Next Step: A funding readiness checklist outlines exactly which financial statements to prepare.

How can entrepreneurs improve their financial literacy?

Short Answer: Entrepreneurs improve financial literacy through structured education (books, courses, workshops), regular review of their own financial statements, and working with advisors or consultants who explain the reasoning behind financial decisions.

Detailed Answer: Financial literacy compounds over time. Reviewing your own numbers monthly, asking lenders and advisors to explain terms and decisions, and engaging with educational content built specifically for entrepreneurs (rather than general personal finance content) accelerates the learning curve.

Capital Readiness

Recommended Page: Home

What is capital readiness?

Short Answer: Capital readiness is the broader state of being prepared to raise or qualify for capital, whether through debt, equity, or grants, encompassing fundability, documentation, financial systems, and strategic positioning.

Detailed Answer: Capital readiness is sometimes used interchangeably with funding readiness, but it often carries a broader scope, including investor readiness for businesses pursuing equity capital, not just lender-based debt financing.

Next Step: See the Funding Readiness FAQs for the lender-specific version of this preparation.

What is investor readiness?

Short Answer: Investor readiness is a business's preparedness to pursue equity investment, including a clear business model, defensible financial projections, a coherent pitch, and clean documentation that can withstand investor due diligence.

Detailed Answer: Investor readiness shares some overlap with funding readiness, clean financials and documentation matter to both, but it also requires a compelling growth narrative and a clear understanding of valuation and ownership structure that debt financing doesn't require.

Next Step: Discuss investor readiness specifically during a consultation if you're pursuing equity capital.

How is capital readiness different from funding readiness?

Short Answer: Funding readiness typically refers to debt-based lending preparation, while capital readiness is a broader term covering readiness for any capital source, including investors, grants, and government contracts, not just loans.

Detailed Answer: In practice, the two terms overlap heavily for most small businesses, since debt financing is the most common capital source. The distinction matters more for businesses considering equity investment or large institutional grants, which require additional preparation beyond standard lender documentation.

Next Step: Clarify which capital sources you're pursuing during a fundability consultation.

508(c)(1)(A)

What is a 508(c)(1)(A) organization?

Short Answer: A 508(c)(1)(A) organization is a faith-based or church-integrated entity that can operate as tax-exempt under the Internal Revenue Code without filing for formal 501(c)(3) recognition, provided it meets specific structural requirements.

Detailed Answer: Unlike 501(c)(3) organizations, 508(c)(1)(A) entities are automatically considered tax-exempt due to their church-integrated or faith-based nature, but they still must meet operational and structural standards to legitimately maintain that status.

Common Mistake: Assuming 508(c)(1)(A) status means no compliance requirements at all. These organizations still have structural and operational standards to maintain.

Next Step: Discuss 508(c)(1)(A) structuring during a consultation or speaking inquiry.

What is the difference between a 501(c)(3) and a 508(c)(1)(A)?

Short Answer: A 501(c)(3) requires formal IRS application and recognition for tax-exempt status, while a 508(c)(1)(A) is automatically tax-exempt for qualifying faith-based or church-integrated organizations without that filing requirement.

Detailed Answer: Both structures can offer tax-exempt benefits, but they differ in filing requirements, public disclosure obligations, and the degree of IRS oversight. The right choice depends on the organization's mission, structure, and how it intends to operate.

Next Step: Speak with Cheryl about which structure fits your faith-based organization.

How do I structure a faith-based organization as a 508(c)(1)(A)?

Short Answer: Structuring a faith-based organization as a 508(c)(1)(A) requires meeting the IRS's qualifying criteria for church-integrated or auxiliary religious organizations and maintaining clear governance, financial, and operational documentation.

Detailed Answer: While the structure avoids formal 501(c)(3) application, organizations still need clear bylaws, defined religious purpose, and sound financial practices to legitimately operate and maintain their status if ever reviewed.

Can a 508(c)(1)(A) organization accept donations?

Detailed Answer: Donors should still confirm the organization's status and documentation, since the automatic nature of 508(c)(1)(A) exemption means there isn't a public IRS determination letter in the same way 501(c)(3) organizations have.

How does revenue growth relate to fundability?

Short Answer: Revenue growth strengthens fundability by giving lenders a stronger track record to evaluate, but consistent, well-documented revenue matters more than rapid revenue alone when it comes to qualifying for funding.

Detailed Answer: Lenders generally favor steady, predictable revenue trends over volatile, rapidly fluctuating revenue, even when the volatile pattern shows higher peaks. Documentation of revenue, not just the revenue itself, is what ultimately supports a funding application.

What revenue documentation do lenders want to see?

Short Answer: Lenders typically want to see bank statements, profit and loss statements, and sometimes merchant processing statements, usually covering the past several months to a few years, depending on the funding type.

Detailed Answer: Clean, consistent documentation that clearly shows revenue trends over time is far more persuasive to underwriters than verbal claims about growth or future projections alone.

Short Answer: Common funding challenges when scaling include outgrowing existing credit limits, needing larger funding amounts than prior history supports, and facing stricter underwriting as loan size increases.

Detailed Answer: As businesses scale, they often need to move from smaller, easier-to-qualify-for funding types (microloans, smaller lines of credit) to larger, more rigorously underwritten options (SBA loans, larger term loans), which require stronger documentation and credit history.
